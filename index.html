Case Study
AI Enablement

How I built an AI tool rollout program that the CEO actually watched.

When a new internal AI platform launched across a 1,000-person organization spanning three continents, there was no adoption plan. Just a tool and an expectation that people would figure it out.

Three regions, different workflows, different levels of tool familiarity, and no shared time zone for live training. The program needed to work asynchronously and actually change behavior, not just produce a completion number.

Built the whole thing solo. Script, Synthesia avatar configuration, slide-by-slide trigger word mapping, screenshot captures, three embedded quiz blocks, SCORM packaging, and LMS deployment. The production pipeline became the repeatable template for every AI tool launch that followed.

The result: VP-level recognition on launch and adopted as the organizational standard for AI enablement. The content was substantive enough that the CEO watched the full module and followed up with questions, which mattered as a signal that something had actually landed.

Phase 1. Analyze
Audience & gap assessment
Identified three distinct learner groups across US, India, and EMEA with different tool familiarity, workflow contexts, and time zone constraints.
Phase 2. Design
Scenario-based module structure
Designed a 24-slide arc with scenario-based learning, three embedded quiz blocks at key retention moments, and a consistent AI presenter across all content.
Phase 3. Develop
Full production pipeline, built solo
Wrote the script, configured the Synthesia avatar, mapped trigger words slide-by-slide, captured screenshots, and packaged as SCORM 1.2 for LMS deployment.
Phase 4. Implement & Evaluate
Global deployment + executive impact
Deployed globally via LMS. VP recognition on launch. CEO watched in full and sent follow-up questions. Adopted as the org standard for AI enablement.
Outcome
A solo-built module that reached 1,000+ employees across 3 regions, earned CEO-level attention, and became the organizational template for all future AI tool rollouts.

The most skipped phase: Evaluate. Most programs measure completion and call it done. But Kirkpatrick Level 3. behavior change on the job. is where real ROI lives. If behavior hasn't changed, the program didn't work.
Application
What separates adoption from awareness.
"We rolled out the tool to everyone. Sent the training link. Completion rate was 94%. But three months later, barely anyone was actually using it.". L&D Manager, post-launch retrospective
  • Awareness is not adoption. A high completion rate on a 10-minute video does not mean behavior has changed. It means people clicked through.
  • Adoption requires opportunity, motivation, and ability. Remove any one of these and the behavior won't stick. regardless of how good the training is.
  • The environment enables or prevents use. If the tool isn't integrated into the workflow, the best training in the world won't move the needle.
Knowledge Check
A new AI summarization tool was rolled out company-wide. Training completion hit 91% in two weeks. But 60 days later, usage data shows only 18% of employees are using it regularly. What does this most likely indicate?
✓  Correct. High completion confirms exposure. not behavior change. Sustainable adoption requires workflow integration, manager reinforcement, and ongoing performance support beyond the initial training event.
Not quite. The completion data actually shows people engaged with the training. The gap between 91% completion and 18% usage is the classic awareness-vs-adoption problem. Training alone rarely changes behavior without workflow integration and reinforcement alongside it.
