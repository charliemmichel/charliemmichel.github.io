The 1:1 Framework for New Managers

A one-page guide to running effective weekly check-ins

This is not a 40-slide deck. This is what you need to know to run a good 1:1. Use the framework. Ask better questions. Document what matters. Everything else is noise.

Do This / Not That

Do This
  • Let your direct report set the agenda. They know what they need to talk about.
  • Ask open questions. "What's on your mind?" beats "Everything good?"
  • Take notes. If you don't write it down, you will forget. They will notice.
  • Follow up on last week's action items. Shows you were listening.
  • End with clarity. Both of you should know who owns what next.
Don't Do This
  • Cancel 1:1s unless absolutely necessary. Consistency matters more than you think.
  • Turn every 1:1 into a status update. That's what Slack is for.
  • Multitask during the meeting. Close your laptop. Put your phone away.
  • Skip the personal check-in to "save time." That's where trust gets built.
  • Wing it. If you show up unprepared, they will stop preparing too.

Questions That Actually Work

Ask these when conversations stall:

  • What's the most important thing you are working on right now?
  • What's blocking you that I can help remove?
  • What feedback do you have for me as your manager?
  • What's one thing you want to get better at in the next quarter?
  • Is there anything we haven't talked about that I should know?

When and How to Document

What to write down:

Always document: Performance concerns (specific examples, dates, impact). Feedback you gave (what you said, their response). Career development goals. Action items with owners and deadlines.

Never document: Personal health information unless they explicitly ask you to note an accommodation. Salary negotiations (those go through HR). Opinions about other team members.

Where to document: Company-approved system (Confluence, Workday, or designated 1:1 tool). Not personal notes, not Slack DMs, not your notebook. If HR or Legal needs it later, they need to be able to find it.

Quick Reference

When to escalate to HR

Harassment or discrimination claims. Mental health crises. Substance abuse concerns. Threats or violence. Anything that makes you think "I should probably tell someone about this." That's the threshold.

When to loop in your manager

Performance issues that might lead to a PIP. Attrition risk for critical roles. Significant conflict between team members. Structural problems you cannot fix alone (budget, headcount, scope).
